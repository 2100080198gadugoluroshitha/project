Polavaram Project

Overview

The Polavaram Project is a multipurpose irrigation project located on the Godavari River in the Indian state of Andhra Pradesh. It is designed to provide irrigation, drinking water, and hydroelectric power to the region. The project is a national priority and has been declared a national project by the Government of India.

History

The Polavaram Project was first proposed in 1941 by the then Madras Presidency. Over the years, the project has seen several revisions and changes in its design and scope. The project received administrative approval in 2004 and has since been under various stages of construction.

Objectives

  • Provide irrigation facilities to 7.2 lakh acres of agricultural land.
  • Supply drinking water to 540 villages and towns in the region.
  • Generate 960 MW of hydroelectric power to meet the energy needs of the state.
  • Facilitate water supply to industries and municipalities.
  • Reduce the flood risk in the Godavari basin, particularly in the districts of West and East Godavari.

Specifications

  • Dam Type: Earth-cum-rock fill dam
  • Height: 150 feet (45.72 meters)
  • Length: 3,600 meters (11,811 feet)
  • Spillway Capacity: 3 million cubic feet per second (85,000 cubic meters per second)
  • Reservoir Capacity: 194 TMC (thousand million cubic feet)
  • Power Generation Capacity: 960 MW

Benefits

The Polavaram Project is expected to transform the agrarian landscape of Andhra Pradesh by bringing additional land under irrigation. The project will also enhance drinking water supply, provide flood control, and generate substantial hydroelectric power, thereby contributing to the overall development and economic growth of the region. Additionally, it aims to improve the socio-economic conditions of the people in the region by providing employment opportunities and boosting agricultural productivity.

Challenges

The Polavaram Project has faced several challenges, including environmental concerns, displacement of people, and legal hurdles. The project involves the submergence of several villages, leading to the displacement of thousands of families. Ensuring proper rehabilitation and resettlement of the affected people remains a significant challenge. Additionally, the project has faced opposition from neighboring states over water-sharing issues.

Current Status

The project is currently in advanced stages of construction, with significant progress made in the construction of the main dam and spillway. Several components of the project, such as the cofferdam, diaphragm wall, and left and right main canals, are nearing completion. The government is actively working to expedite the remaining construction activities and make the project operational at the earliest.
